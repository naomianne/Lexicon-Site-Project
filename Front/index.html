About The Project

What is the LSA?

The Lexicon of Science in Asia (LSA) aims to enable new transregional and comparative research in the history and sociology of science by helping scholars discover translation histories for scientific terms and concepts.

It is a fully searchable, multilingual database of scientific terms in Asian languages spoken across the continent, from the Eastern Mediterranean to the Pacific Ocean. The Lexicon will bridge gaps in regional language expertise and improve our understanding about how scientific knowledge and technology have been disseminated, translated, and adapted across different parts of Asia.

Dr. Elise K. Burton
Assistant Professor at the Institute for the History and Philosophy of Science and Technology.

She is a historian of the life sciences in the modern Middle East, specializing in genetics, evolutionary biology, physical anthropology, and medicine during the twentieth and twenty-first centuries. She is the author of Genetic Crossroads: The Middle East and the Science of Human Heredity (Stanford University Press, 2021). Her latest project examines scientific connections between the Middle East and South and East Asia. Tentatively called “Race Across Asia,” this project traces the flow of scientific ideas and research practices surrounding race and nationalism between Japan, India, Iran, and Turkey, looking specifically at the fields of medical genetics, forensic fingerprinting, and archaeology. Qieyi Liu
PhD candidate in East Asian Studies at the University of Toronto, specializing in modern Chinese history and culture

Her dissertation project explores the convoluted encounters between translated natural sciences and literati/folk knowledge and practices of nature in late-nineteenth and early-twentieth centuries China, and uncovers a “nature-cultural lexicon” that invoked more relational and culture-mediated ways of knowing and inhabiting the more-than-human world. More broadly, she is interested in diverse ways of more-than-human entanglements in historical archives, ethnographies, fictions, and everyday practices.
